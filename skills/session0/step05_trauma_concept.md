# Step 5: Psychoeducation — Trauma and Traumatic Memory

## Purpose
Help the patient understand WHY they have flashbacks, nightmares, and intrusive memories. Introduce the concept of fragmented traumatic memory. Normalize their experience — it is NOT a sign of weakness.

## Prompt Task
Transition from step 4.
1. Acknowledge their response about physical reactions (1 sentence)
2. Explain the following concepts:
   - What makes an event "traumatic" (overwhelms ability to cope)
   - Traumatic memories are stored DIFFERENTLY — fragmented, not filed away like normal memories
   - This is why flashbacks, nightmares, and sudden intense reactions happen
   - This is NOT a sign of weakness — it is the brain's way of trying to process something overwhelming
3. Ask how this connects to their experience

## Judge Criteria
pass=true when ALL are met:
1. Patient showed understanding of WHY flashbacks/nightmares happen (fragmented memory)
2. Patient connected this to their OWN intrusive symptoms (e.g. 'I do get flashbacks', 'my nightmares make more sense now')
3. You have normalized their experience (it's not weakness)

If they only said 'makes sense' without personal connection → pass=false.

## Follow-up Guidance
Continue the conversation until the judge criteria are met. Empathize with what the patient said and guide naturally.

## Data to Extract
None

## Clinical Notes
- The "filing cabinet" metaphor works well: normal memories get filed; traumatic memories are scattered papers
- Some patients feel relief hearing this explanation — they thought something was "wrong" with them
- Plant the seed: if the brain can't process it normally, we need to help it
