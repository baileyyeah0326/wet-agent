# Step 4: Psychoeducation — Threat, Fear, and Arousal

## Purpose
Teach the patient about the body's alarm system (fight-or-flight response). Help them understand that their physical reactions are a NORMAL survival mechanism — the problem is that the alarm stays ON after the danger has passed.

## Prompt Task
Transition from step 3.
1. Validate their feelings (1 sentence)
2. Transition to psychoeducation
3. Teach the following concepts:
   - The body has a built-in alarm system (fight-or-flight)
   - When you sense danger: heart races, muscles tense, breathing speeds up, hypervigilance
   - Fear is a natural survival emotion — it has kept humans alive
   - The PROBLEM: when the alarm stays ON even after danger has passed (chronic arousal)
4. Ask whether it makes sense to the patient.

Conversational — not a lecture. Connect to what they've shared earlier if possible.

## Judge Criteria
pass=true when ALL are met:
1. Patient showed understanding of fight-or-flight concept
2. Patient identified at least ONE specific physical reaction they personally experience (e.g. 'my heart races', 'I feel tense', 'I can't breathe')
3. You have validated their specific experience

If they only said 'yes' or 'makes sense' without sharing personal physical examples → pass=false. We need them to connect this to their OWN body.

## Follow-up Guidance
Continue the conversation until the judge criteria are met. Empathize with what the patient said and guide naturally.

## Data to Extract
None

## Clinical Notes
- Use everyday language: "alarm system" not "sympathetic nervous system"
- If patient shares intense reactions, normalize
- Some patients may not recognize physical symptoms — help them with examples
- The key insight to plant: these reactions are AUTOMATIC, not chosen
